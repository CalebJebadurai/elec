# Refined Prompt: Surveyor-Centric Prediction Platform V2

**Date:** 2026-04-30  
**Status:** Research Phase  
**Scope:** Full-Stack — Data Science + Backend + Frontend + Collaboration Infrastructure  
**Context:** V1 Delivered and Operational

---

## Problem Statement

The V1 multi-factor prediction system successfully delivers twelve adjustable sliders, formula-based and ML-based prediction modes, alliance configuration, and CSV survey import capabilities. However, it fundamentally remains an **analyst tool** where expert users adjust abstract factor sliders based on intuition, domain knowledge, or manually transcribed survey data. The critical gap is that field surveyors—the people actually collecting ground intelligence through booth-level canvassing, voter sentiment tracking, and door-to-door surveys—have no direct path to input their findings into the prediction engine. The current CSV import mechanism requires technical sophistication (understanding factor names, value ranges, CSV formatting) and provides no validation, confidence tracking, survey progress management, or collaborative workflows. The system assumes all survey data arrives complete and correct in batch, rather than incrementally and collaboratively from distributed survey teams working in real-time across hundreds of constituencies. Additionally, predictions remain constituency-level aggregates with no visibility into booth-level dynamics, poll-by-poll variation, or micro-geographic patterns that surveyors observe on the ground. V2 must transform the platform from an analyst sandbox into a **surveyor-first prediction engine** where field teams directly enter observations, the system validates and tracks data quality, predictions update in real-time as surveys progress, and both surveyors and analysts gain actionable insights from granular booth-level forecasts integrated with advanced visualization, scenario analysis, and psephological reporting capabilities.

## Current State

The V1 system (fully implemented and operational) consists of a FastAPI backend serving election data from PostgreSQL (TCPD datasets for all Indian states covering Assembly Elections, General Elections, and General Assembly data), a React frontend with Vite build tooling, and a prediction engine with two operational modes. The formula-based mode runs client-side in TypeScript at [frontend/src/engine/predictionEngine.ts](frontend/src/engine/predictionEngine.ts), applying multiplicative proportional swing adjustments based on twelve factor sliders (turnoutChange, incumbencyFatigue, turncoatPenalty, recontestBonus, sameConstituencyBonus, previousMarginFactor, enopFactor, nCandFactor, constituencyTypeFactor, genderFactor, partyStrengthFactor, partyVoteShareFactor). Each factor has validated ranges, state-specific regression coefficients stored in [frontend/src/engine/data/coefficients.json](frontend/src/engine/data/coefficients.json), and a tooltip explaining its effect. The ML-based mode runs server-side via ONNX model inference at the `/v1/predict/ml` endpoint implemented in [api/prediction_routes.py](api/prediction_routes.py), returning constituency-level predictions with analytic confidence intervals computed from quantile regression and variance propagation assuming block-correlated constituency swings. Alliance configuration is fully functional with user-editable alliance blocs, pre-populated presets for major states stored in [frontend/src/engine/data/alliance_data.json](frontend/src/engine/data/alliance_data.json), and configurable vote transfer efficiency factors. Survey data import exists via [SurveyImportModal.tsx](frontend/src/components/SurveyImportModal.tsx), which accepts CSV files with (factor, value) rows, validates against factor catalog ranges, and populates slider overrides. The UI implements mobile-responsive collapsible sections for factor categories, displays error margins as seat count ranges with confidence levels, and provides formula/ML mode toggle. Bookmarks preserve prediction configurations in JSONB with backward compatibility for legacy three-parameter bookmarks. Data science notebooks at [datascience/notebooks/08_factor_discovery.ipynb](datascience/notebooks/08_factor_discovery.ipynb) document the research pipeline that derived factor coefficients and trained the XGBoost model. The database schema at [api/models.py](api/models.py) includes candidate-level fields (age, sex, party, votes, incumbent, turncoat, recontest, no_terms), constituency-level fields (constituency_name, constituency_no, constituency_type, district_name, sub_region, n_cand, turnout_percentage, enop), and state-level fields (state_name, assembly_no, year). No booth-level data, surveyor management, survey campaign tracking, real-time dashboards, collaboration workflows, or granular geographic analysis capabilities exist in the current system.

## Desired End State

The V2 system should enable field survey teams to conduct election ground intelligence operations with full end-to-end digital support, from survey campaign planning through booth-level data collection to real-time predictive analytics and psephological report generation. A survey coordinator should be able to create a survey campaign for an upcoming election, define the geographic scope (entire state or selected constituencies), assign surveyor accounts to specific booth clusters, and configure survey questionnaires with custom fields beyond the standard factor sliders (e.g., dominant local issue, scheme satisfaction scores, candidate likeability ratings, caste/community sentiment where culturally appropriate and legally permissible). Field surveyors using mobile devices should access a streamlined data entry interface where they select their assigned booth, input observations through validated forms with range checks and mandatory field enforcement, attach photos or voice notes as evidence, rate their confidence in each observation (high/medium/low based on sample size and consistency), and submit entries incrementally throughout the day with offline-first support and background sync. The backend should receive survey submissions via a new survey API, validate entries against campaign configuration, store booth-level raw data in a dedicated survey schema separate from historical TCPD data, compute data quality scores for each booth and surveyor based on completion rate, consistency across overlapping observations, and confidence ratings, and trigger prediction recalculations automatically when sufficient new data arrives for a constituency. The prediction engine should be extended to operate at booth-level granularity, aggregating booth predictions to constituency and state totals while preserving visibility into poll-by-poll variation and micro-geographic patterns. Predictions should blend historical TCPD baselines with live survey data using a Bayesian updating framework where survey observations progressively override historical priors as data quality and coverage improve, with transparent confidence intervals that narrow as survey completion increases and widen when survey data contradicts historical patterns. The frontend should provide a real-time survey dashboard showing campaign progress (booths surveyed vs. total booths, completion percentage by constituency, surveyor productivity metrics, data quality scores), a constituency heat map visualizing prediction confidence and swing magnitude with color-coded overlays, an interactive scenario comparison tool allowing analysts to save multiple prediction configurations and view side-by-side differences in seat counts and vote shares, and a psephological report generator that produces PDF documents with executive summaries, key findings, swing analysis, demographic breakdowns, and data provenance notes suitable for media publication or campaign strategy use. The system should integrate external data sources where feasible and valuable: social media sentiment scores aggregated by constituency from Twitter/X and regional platforms using keyword and hashtag tracking, weather and logistics factors (monsoon forecasts, road connectivity indices) that affect turnout, development indices from publicly available NITI Aayog or Census data correlated with voting patterns, and caste/community demographic estimates where such data is legally accessible and the surveyor chooses to collect it. Advanced ML capabilities should include per-prediction SHAP explanations showing the top five factors driving each constituency's forecast, real-time model retraining triggered when survey data volume exceeds a threshold, ensemble methods combining multiple model architectures weighted by held-out validation performance, and anomaly detection flagging constituencies where survey data diverges dramatically from historical patterns (potential data quality issues or genuine electoral realignments requiring manual review). All interactive visualizations should be accessible (ARIA-compliant), mobile-responsive, and performant (sub-second rendering for state-level maps and charts). Multi-surveyor collaboration should include conflict resolution workflows when multiple surveyors submit contradictory data for the same booth, admin dashboards for survey coordinators to review flagged entries and override data quality scores, and audit logs tracking all survey submissions and edits with timestamps and user attribution. The system should maintain strict data access controls where survey campaigns can be marked private (visible only to the creating organization and assigned surveyors) or public (predictions visible to all users, but raw survey data redacted). The enhanced platform should work seamlessly for all Indian states with Assembly Election data, gracefully degrading to formula-only predictions for states with insufficient historical data for ML model training, and should preserve full backward compatibility with V1 bookmarks and prediction workflows so existing analyst users experience V2 as an additive upgrade rather than a disruptive migration.

## Scope Definition

**In Scope:**

1. **Survey Campaign Management Backend:** Create new database schema with tables for `survey_campaigns` (campaign_id, creator_user_id, state, election_year, scope_constituencies, start_date, end_date, status, questionnaire_config JSONB), `survey_booths` (booth_id, campaign_id, constituency_no, booth_number, booth_name, geolocation, assigned_surveyors, target_sample_size), `survey_submissions` (submission_id, booth_id, surveyor_user_id, timestamp, observations JSONB, confidence_scores JSONB, evidence_attachments, quality_score, reviewed_status), and `surveyor_accounts` (user_id foreign key, surveyor_role, assigned_campaigns, performance_metrics JSONB). Implement API endpoints in a new `survey_routes.py` file: POST `/v1/surveys/campaigns` to create campaigns, GET `/v1/surveys/campaigns/{id}` to retrieve campaign configuration and progress, POST `/v1/surveys/booths/{booth_id}/submit` to accept surveyor submissions with Pydantic validation, GET `/v1/surveys/dashboard/{campaign_id}` to return aggregated progress metrics and quality scores, PUT `/v1/surveys/submissions/{id}/review` for admin override and flagging. All endpoints require authentication with role-based access control distinguishing campaign creators, assigned surveyors, and admin reviewers.

2. **Booth-Level Data Model and Aggregation:** Extend the prediction engine to operate on booth-level units. Since TCPD data is constituency-level, booth-level predictions for historical baselines require synthetic disaggregation: split each constituency's historical vote totals proportionally across booths based on assumed uniform distribution as a naive baseline, or where survey campaigns provide booth-level voter list sizes, use actual booth elector counts for proportional disaggregation. Survey observations at booth-level (turnout estimates, party support percentages, sentiment scores) are stored as booth-specific overrides. The prediction pipeline computes booth-level forecasts by applying factor adjustments to the disaggregated historical baseline and survey overrides, then aggregates booth predictions to constituency totals using weighted sums, and finally computes state-level totals. Booth-level granularity is exposed via a new API endpoint GET `/v1/predict/booths/{constituency_no}` returning an array of booth predictions with booth_id, predicted_winner, vote_shares, and confidence intervals. This endpoint is computationally expensive (10x-20x more predictions than constituency-level) and is restricted to authenticated users with rate limiting of five requests per minute.

3. **Surveyor Mobile-First UI:** Build a new React component suite under `frontend/src/components/survey/` optimized for mobile touchscreens and offline-first operation. Components include `SurveyCampaignSelector` (list assigned campaigns, select active campaign), `BoothSelector` (map view or list view of assigned booths with completion status indicators), `SurveyDataEntryForm` (dynamic form generated from campaign's questionnaire_config with field types: numeric sliders, categorical dropdowns, Likert scales, free-text notes, photo upload), `ConfidenceRating` (three-level selector for each observation), and `SubmitButton` with offline queue indicator. Use IndexedDB via Dexie.js for offline storage of draft submissions, service workers for background sync when network reconnects, and optimistic UI updates showing submissions as "pending sync" until server confirmation. Validation errors are surfaced immediately before submission with clear field-level messages. Photo uploads are compressed to under 500KB per image before transmission and stored in a new S3-compatible object storage bucket (implementation uses local filesystem for development, Railway's persistent volume or Cloudflare R2 for production). The UI tracks surveyor location via browser Geolocation API (with explicit permission prompting) and attaches GPS coordinates to submissions for quality verification (detecting if surveyor is actually at the booth or entering data remotely).

4. **Real-Time Survey Dashboard:** Create a new `SurveyDashboard.tsx` component accessible to campaign creators and admin users, displaying: campaign header with total booths, surveyed booths, completion percentage, days remaining; constituency-level progress table sortable by completion percentage with drill-down to booth list; surveyor leaderboard showing submissions per surveyor, average quality scores, and flagged entries count; data quality chart visualizing distribution of confidence ratings and consistency scores; live activity feed showing recent submissions with surveyor name, booth, timestamp. The dashboard polls the backend every thirty seconds for updates via GET `/v1/surveys/dashboard/{campaign_id}` endpoint returning delta updates since last poll to minimize bandwidth. Use React Query with staleTime configuration to cache dashboard data and avoid redundant fetches. The dashboard is mobile-responsive but optimized for tablet and desktop viewports (surveyors use the simplified mobile entry UI, coordinators use the dashboard on laptops).

5. **Interactive Constituency Heat Map:** Extend the existing `IndiaMap.jsx` component to support new visualization modes: "Survey Progress" mode color-coding constituencies by completion percentage (red < 25%, yellow 25-75%, green > 75%), "Prediction Confidence" mode color-coding by error margin width (narrow = high confidence = dark blue, wide = low confidence = light blue), "Swing Magnitude" mode color-coding by absolute swing from previous election (large swings = red/orange, small swings = yellow/green). Use D3.js topojson rendering with zoom and pan controls, clickable constituencies triggering a sidebar panel showing detailed predictions, survey progress, and booth-level breakdown. The map component fetches geojson boundaries from a new static asset `public/maps/india-constituencies.json` (this file does not exist yet and must be sourced from Election Commission shapefiles or third-party datasets like DataMeet India maps). Rendering performance for 543 Lok Sabha constituencies or 4000+ Assembly constituencies across all states requires canvas-based rendering or WebGL acceleration using deck.gl instead of SVG—this is a known performance constraint and fallback to state-level maps may be necessary if constituency-level rendering exceeds the 500ms target.

6. **Scenario Comparison Tool:** Build a `ScenarioComparison.tsx` component allowing analysts to save multiple prediction configurations (slider settings, alliance configurations, survey data selections) as named scenarios, compare two or three scenarios side-by-side in a tabular view showing per-party seat counts and vote share differences, and visualize scenario divergence with bar charts and diff highlights. Scenarios are stored in the bookmarks table with a new `scenario_group_id` field linking related scenarios. The comparison view computes delta metrics (scenario A vs B: +5 seats for Party X, -2.3pp vote share for Party Y) and color-codes significant differences (>5 seats or >3pp vote share = bold red/green). This feature targets power users and may be hidden behind a feature flag initially to avoid cluttering the UI for casual users.

7. **Psephological Report Generator:** Implement a backend service at POST `/v1/reports/generate` accepting a campaign_id or prediction configuration and returning a PDF document generated using a Python library such as ReportLab or WeasyPrint. The report structure includes: title page with election name and date, executive summary with headline seat projections and key swing constituencies, methodology section explaining factor adjustments and data sources, state-level summary with party-wise seat counts and vote shares, constituency-level detailed results table, swing analysis section identifying top gainers and losers, demographic breakdown if survey data includes relevant fields, data provenance section listing survey completion rates and confidence scores, and disclaimer footer. Report generation is computationally expensive (5-10 seconds for a state with 200+ constituencies) and queued via a Celery task queue (or simpler synchronous generation with extended timeout for low-traffic personal project context). Generated PDFs are stored in object storage with a seven-day expiration and returned via a signed URL.

8. **Social Media Sentiment Integration:** Build a sentiment analysis pipeline ingesting Twitter/X data via API (requires API access tier with search capability, cost approximately $100/month for basic tier) or free alternative using RSS feeds and scraped public timelines (legally uncertain, blocked by rate limits, not recommended for production). The pipeline aggregates tweets mentioning party names, candidate names, or election-related hashtags, classifies sentiment using a pre-trained model such as IndicBERT fine-tuned on political sentiment or a simpler VADER lexicon adapted for Indian English and regional language transliteration, and computes constituency-level sentiment scores by geotagging tweets or inferring location from user bios. Sentiment scores are normalized to a -1 (negative) to +1 (positive) scale and exposed as a new factor "Social Media Sentiment" in the prediction engine. This feature has high data quality uncertainty (bots, astroturfing, urban bias in Twitter usage) and should be marked experimental with large error margins. Given the user is a single developer on a personal project, this feature is a candidate for deferral unless free data sources (e.g., public sentiment datasets from Kaggle or academic researchers) are available.

9. **Weather and Logistics Factors:** Integrate publicly available weather forecast data from India Meteorological Department APIs or international providers like OpenWeatherMap to compute monsoon intensity forecasts for election dates by constituency, flagging constituencies with predicted heavy rainfall that historically correlate with reduced turnout. Integrate road connectivity indices from OpenStreetMap data or government datasets to compute an accessibility score for each constituency (higher = easier voter access = higher turnout), particularly relevant for hilly or remote regions. These factors are added to the factor catalog as "Weather Impact" and "Accessibility Index" sliders with defaults derived from historical correlation analysis. Data freshness is critical—weather forecasts are only valid for 7-10 days ahead, so this feature is useful for last-week predictions but not long-range forecasts. This is a lower-priority enhancement suitable for a later sub-release.

10. **Development Index Integration:** Correlate constituency-level predictions with publicly available development indicators from NITI Aayog's Aspirational Districts Programme, Census 2011 socio-economic data, or UNDP India datasets. Compute a composite development index per district (constituencies are mapped to districts via the existing `district_name` field in TCPD data), use this index as a new factor "Development Disparity Effect" hypothesizing that underdeveloped districts show stronger anti-incumbency when government is perceived to have failed on development. Validate this hypothesis in the data science phase by computing correlation between development indices and incumbent party performance across historical elections. If correlation is statistically significant (p < 0.05, |coefficient| > 0.1), include the factor; otherwise document as "tested but not predictive" and exclude. This is a research-first feature requiring data acquisition and validation before implementation.

11. **Advanced ML Enhancements:** Extend the existing ONNX model inference with per-prediction SHAP explanations computed at runtime by running a fast approximate SHAP library (shap.TreeExplainer for tree-based models has near-zero marginal cost per prediction). Return the top five contributing factors for each constituency as part of the `/v1/predict/ml` response in a new `feature_attributions` field. Implement real-time model retraining triggered when a survey campaign accumulates more than 1000 booth-level submissions—the retraining job pulls the new survey data, augments the training set, retrains the XGBoost model with the same hyperparameters, validates on held-out constituencies, and if validation accuracy exceeds the current model's accuracy, deploys the new model by updating the `model_metadata` table and replacing the ONNX file. Retraining is expensive (10-30 minutes depending on dataset size) and should be queued as a background task, not blocking API responses. Ensemble methods combine predictions from three models (Random Forest, XGBoost, LightGBM) trained independently, weighted by their held-out validation accuracy per state, and return a blended prediction with variance computed from inter-model disagreement. Anomaly detection flags constituencies where the ML prediction diverges from the formula prediction by more than 15 percentage points or 10 seats, triggering a manual review queue for data scientists to investigate.

12. **Booth-Level Granularity Data Sourcing:** Since TCPD datasets do not include booth-level historical data, booth-level predictions require either (a) synthetic disaggregation from constituency totals as described above, or (b) integration of booth-level historical data from Election Commission of India's statistical reports. ECI publishes booth-wise results for some states and election years as PDF tables, which can be scraped using Tabula or Camelot Python libraries and ingested into a new `booth_results` table. This data acquisition and cleaning is time-intensive (50+ hours for all states) and error-prone (OCR quality varies, table formats inconsistent across states), making it a high-effort, high-value workstream suitable for phased rollout (start with one state like Tamil Nadu, validate the pipeline, then scale to other states). Alternatively, partner with organizations like Lok Sabha or Datameet India that may have cleaned booth-level datasets, though data sharing agreements may be required.

13. **Caste and Community Demographic Modeling:** Where legally permissible and culturally appropriate, allow survey campaigns to collect caste/community sentiment data (e.g., "dominant community's party preference" or "SC/ST voter enthusiasm score") as custom questionnaire fields. This data is highly sensitive and must comply with Indian election laws (Model Code of Conduct restrictions on caste-based appeals) and data protection regulations. The system should provide clear consent prompts explaining how the data will be used, anonymize individual responses (aggregate to booth-level minimums of 10 responses), and allow surveyors to opt out of collecting such data. Given the legal and ethical complexity, this feature should be designed with input from legal counsel and election law experts, and may be disabled by default with opt-in for organizations with explicit research or journalistic mandates. For the personal project context, this is a candidate for deferral unless the user has domain expertise and legal confidence.

14. **Collaboration and Conflict Resolution:** When multiple surveyors submit observations for the same booth and factor (e.g., Surveyor A reports 55% turnout, Surveyor B reports 62% turnout for Booth 123), the system flags the conflict in the survey dashboard and computes a weighted average based on surveyor confidence ratings and historical quality scores. Surveyors with higher average quality scores (fewer flagged entries, more consistent with final outcomes in past campaigns) have their observations weighted more heavily. Campaign coordinators can manually review conflicts via a `/surveys/conflicts` admin view, see both submissions side-by-side with evidence attachments, and select one submission as authoritative or enter a manually reconciled value. All conflict resolutions are logged in an audit trail. This workflow prevents data quality degradation from careless or malicious surveyor entries.

15. **Testing and Validation:** Extend the existing frontend test suite (Vitest + jsdom) and backend test suite (pytest) to cover survey workflows: end-to-end test simulating a survey campaign creation, booth assignment, surveyor submission, and prediction update; unit tests for booth-level disaggregation and aggregation logic; API integration tests for survey endpoints with authentication and authorization checks; UI tests for offline-first behavior using service worker mocks. Validate booth-level predictions by backtesting: for states where booth-level historical data is available, compute booth-level predictions using a held-out election year, aggregate to constituency totals, and verify that accuracy matches or exceeds constituency-level predictions (proving that booth granularity does not introduce excessive noise). Performance test the survey dashboard and heat map rendering under load (1000+ booths, 50+ surveyors, real-time updates) to ensure sub-three-second load times. Accessibility audit the surveyor mobile UI using axe-core and manual screen reader testing to ensure WCAG 2.1 AA compliance.

**Out of Scope:**

1. **Live Exit Poll Data Integration:** Real-time ingestion of exit poll results on election day from news agencies or polling organizations is not in scope. Exit polls are proprietary, expensive, and subject to strict embargo regulations under Indian election law (results cannot be published until voting concludes). This would require commercial partnerships and legal compliance beyond a personal project's capacity. V2 focuses on pre-election survey data and ground intelligence, not election-day exit polling.

2. **Booth-Level Results Publication on Election Day:** While the system can generate booth-level predictions before an election, it will not scrape or ingest live booth-level counting results during election day result announcements. This would require real-time web scraping of ECI result dashboards or news tickers, robust error handling for format changes, and high-availability infrastructure to handle traffic spikes. This is a separate initiative (a V3 "Election Night Dashboard") and is explicitly deferred.

3. **Historical Booth-Level Data for All States:** As noted in the in-scope section, integrating booth-level historical results for all Indian states from ECI PDFs is a 50+ hour data acquisition workstream. V2 will implement the booth-level prediction architecture and demonstrate it with synthetic disaggregation and one pilot state (Tamil Nadu if data is readily available). Full national booth-level historical data integration is deferred to a follow-on data enrichment project.

4. **Video Analysis and Deepfake Detection:** Some advanced election monitoring systems analyze campaign videos, rally footage, or deepfake detection for misinformation tracking. This is completely out of scope for V2—it requires computer vision infrastructure, video storage, and content moderation capabilities far beyond the current system's focus on numerical predictions and survey data.

5. **Blockchain-Based Audit Trails:** While audit logs and data provenance are in scope using conventional PostgreSQL logging and timestamp tracking, implementing blockchain or distributed ledger technology for tamper-proof survey submission tracking is out of scope. This adds architectural complexity without clear user-facing value for a single-organization deployment model (blockchain's trustlessness property is unnecessary when the platform operator controls all infrastructure).

6. **Multi-Language Surveyor UI:** The surveyor mobile UI will initially launch in English only. Adding Hindi, Tamil, Telugu, Bengali, and other regional languages for surveyor-facing components (data entry forms, tooltips, validation messages) is valuable but requires translation infrastructure, Unicode handling validation, and region-specific testing. This is deferred to a post-V2 internationalization initiative. The public-facing analyst UI (existing V1 components) may already have some multi-language support; that is preserved but not extended.

7. **Machine Learning Model Explainability Beyond SHAP:** Advanced explainability techniques like LIME, counterfactual explanations, or model-agnostic gradient-based attribution are not included. SHAP feature attributions provide sufficient transparency for the target user base. More sophisticated explainability would primarily serve academic researchers and is deferred.

8. **Integration with External Survey Tools:** The system does not integrate with third-party survey platforms like SurveySparrow, Typeform, or ODK Collect. Surveyors must use the native survey UI built into the platform. Bidirectional sync with external tools would require OAuth integrations, data mapping transformations, and ongoing maintenance as external APIs evolve. This is out of scope for an integrated platform where control over the survey workflow is a design requirement for data quality assurance.

## Constraints and Assumptions

**Assumptions:**

1. **Surveyor Adoption and Training:** The system assumes survey coordinators will provide training to field surveyors on using the mobile data entry interface, understanding factor definitions, and applying confidence ratings. Users without training may submit low-quality data (e.g., guessing values, misunderstanding factors, inflating confidence). The system mitigates this with clear UI tooltips, validation errors, and quality scoring, but cannot eliminate user error. If pilot deployments reveal high error rates, additional training materials (video tutorials, in-app walkthroughs) may be required.

2. **Network Connectivity for Surveyors:** While offline-first support is in scope (surveyors can enter data without connectivity and sync later), the system assumes surveyors will achieve network connectivity at least once per day to upload submissions. Rural areas with zero connectivity (no mobile data, no Wi-Fi) for days at a time are edge cases that may require surveyor-to-coordinator USB data transfer workflows not supported by the web app. Survey coordinators should assess connectivity before deploying surveyors to extremely remote booths.

3. **Booth-Level Prediction Validity:** The assumption that booth-level disaggregation from constituency-level historical data produces meaningful predictions is untested. Constituencies are not homogeneous—some booths are urban, some rural; some are dominated by one community, others are mixed. Uniform disaggregation (splitting a constituency's 100,000 votes evenly across 100 booths = 1,000 votes per booth) is a naive baseline that ignores these variations. If actual booth-level historical data reveals high variance in booth voting patterns within a constituency, the disaggregation-based predictions will have wide error margins and limited utility. This risk is mitigated by prioritizing states where booth-level historical data is available for validation.

4. **Survey Data Quality and Honesty:** The system assumes most surveyors are acting in good faith and making honest observations. Malicious actors (e.g., surveyors working for a political party intentionally skewing data to manipulate predictions) could submit false data. The data quality scoring, multi-surveyor conflict detection, and admin review workflows provide some protection, but are not foolproof. Survey campaigns marked as "public" (predictions visible to all users) should be reviewed by coordinators before publication to avoid spreading disinformation.

5. **Legal Compliance with Election Laws:** The system assumes that collecting and publishing pre-election predictions based on survey data is legally permissible under Indian election law. The Model Code of Conduct restricts certain activities (paid news, caste-based appeals, misleading information) but does not, to the best of the author's knowledge, prohibit private organizations from conducting surveys and generating predictions. However, this is not a legal opinion, and the user should consult election law experts if deploying publicly during an active election under the Model Code of Conduct. Predictions should carry disclaimers that they are not official and are for informational purposes only.

6. **TCPD Data Recency:** The system assumes the TCPD datasets (`data/TCPD_AE_All_States_2026-4-30.csv.gz` and related files) are updated with recent elections and will continue to be updated as new elections occur. If TCPD stops maintaining the datasets or the user does not update them, predictions for future elections will be based on increasingly stale historical patterns. A V2 feature could add an admin interface for ingesting ECI result data directly, but this is out of scope for the initial release. The user should plan to refresh TCPD datasets at least annually.

7. **Social Media Sentiment Representativeness:** If social media sentiment integration is implemented, the system assumes Twitter/X activity is a proxy for broader voter sentiment. In reality, Twitter users in India skew urban, young, English-speaking, and higher-income compared to the general electorate. Sentiment scores derived from social media will overrepresent these demographics and may poorly predict rural or lower-income voter behavior. Error margins for predictions using social media factors should be widened to reflect this uncertainty, and the factor should be labeled "Urban Social Media Sentiment" to set appropriate expectations.

8. **Development Index Correlation Holds Across Time:** If development index integration is included, the system assumes that the relationship between a constituency's development level and its voting behavior (e.g., "underdeveloped districts punish incumbents more harshly") observed in historical data will continue to hold in future elections. Structural changes (e.g., new welfare schemes that dramatically improve development indicators, or rising polarization that makes development less salient to voters) could break this relationship. The factor should be retested with each new election cycle.

9. **User Authentication and Security:** The system assumes the existing authentication system (based on [api/auth_routes.py](api/auth_routes.py) and [api/auth.py](api/auth.py)) is secure and that surveyor accounts are only created by trusted survey coordinators. If an attacker compromises a surveyor account, they could submit fraudulent survey data. Two-factor authentication for surveyor accounts and rate limiting on survey submission endpoints (e.g., max 100 submissions per surveyor per day) provide additional security layers and are recommended but not strictly required for initial release.

10. **Object Storage Availability:** Survey photo uploads require object storage (local filesystem for development, Railway persistent volume or Cloudflare R2 for production). The system assumes the storage backend is reliable and has sufficient capacity. A production deployment with 100 surveyors uploading 10 photos per day at 500KB each requires approximately 500MB/day or 15GB/month, well within typical storage plan limits. If usage spikes, automatic cleanup of photos older than 90 days should be implemented to control costs.

**Constraints:**

1. **Single Developer Personal Project:** This is a personal project with one developer, not a funded team. The scope must be realistic for one person to implement over 3-6 months. The phased implementation plan prioritizes core surveyor workflows (Phases 1-3) and treats advanced features (social media sentiment, development indices, booth-level historical data for all states) as optional stretch goals or deferred work. The user should adjust scope dynamically based on progress and complexity encountered during implementation.

2. **No Budget for Commercial APIs or Data:** The project has no budget for paid API access (e.g., Twitter Enterprise API at $5,000+/month, premium weather APIs, commercial satellite imagery). All external data integrations must use free tiers, open datasets, or academic partnerships. This may limit data quality or feature feasibility, and the user should validate data source availability before committing to a feature.

3. **Performance Constraints on Booth-Level Predictions:** Booth-level predictions are computationally expensive. A state like Uttar Pradesh has approximately 403 constituencies with an average of 200-300 booths per constituency, totaling 80,000+ booths. Computing predictions for all booths requires 80,000 prediction evaluations (formula mode) or 80,000 model inferences (ML mode). Formula mode on the client side may take 10-20 seconds for a full-state computation (acceptable if triggered manually by user, not automatically on every slider adjustment). ML mode on the backend would require model batching and may take 30-60 seconds even with optimized ONNX inference (likely too slow for real-time UI, requiring pre-computation and caching). The implementation must include performance budgets: booth-level predictions are only computed on-demand for a single constituency (200-300 booths, sub-3-second target) or pre-computed overnight for full-state maps.

4. **Database Storage Growth:** Survey submissions accumulate indefinitely and include JSONB observations, confidence scores, and photo URLs. A large survey campaign (1000 booths × 5 submissions per booth average × 10KB per submission) requires 50MB per campaign. Multiple campaigns per state per year across multiple states could reach gigabytes. The PostgreSQL database on Railway's free tier has a 1GB limit; the user should plan to upgrade to a paid plan ($5-10/month for 10GB) or implement data archival (move campaigns older than 1 year to cold storage) to manage growth.

5. **Mobile Browser Compatibility:** The surveyor mobile UI must work on Android (Chrome, Samsung Internet) and iOS (Safari) browsers, which have varying support for service workers, IndexedDB, geolocation, and media upload. The implementation should use progressive enhancement (core functionality works everywhere, advanced features like offline mode gracefully degrade if unsupported) and test on real devices, not just desktop browser emulators. Known issue: iOS Safari has restrictive service worker behavior (limited cache size, eviction during inactivity) that may reduce offline capability.

6. **Geojson Constituency Boundaries:** The interactive heat map requires geojson boundary files for Indian constituencies. Election Commission shapefiles are available but require conversion to geojson and simplification (full-resolution boundaries are too large for web delivery, files can reach 50MB+). Simplified geojson at ~1MB per state is acceptable. DataMeet India provides some preprocessed files; the user should verify licensing (typically CC-BY, allowing use with attribution) and spatial accuracy (boundaries align with current delimitation) before use.

7. **Report Generation Latency:** PDF report generation for a full state may take 5-10 seconds on the backend (ReportLab rendering). This blocks the API request unless queued as a background task. For a personal project without Celery/Redis infrastructure, synchronous generation with an extended timeout (30 seconds) is acceptable, but the frontend must show a loading spinner and disable the "Generate Report" button to prevent duplicate requests. Users should expect delays for large reports.

8. **No Real-Time WebSocket Infrastructure:** The real-time survey dashboard uses polling (GET `/v1/surveys/dashboard/{id}` every 30 seconds) rather than WebSockets or Server-Sent Events. This is simpler to implement and sufficient for surveyor-scale workloads (dozens of active surveyors, not thousands). True real-time updates with sub-second latency would require WebSocket infrastructure (e.g., Socket.IO, Django Channels) and persistent connections, increasing backend complexity and resource usage. Polling is the pragmatic choice for V2.

9. **Backward Compatibility Preservation:** All existing V1 bookmarks, API endpoints, and prediction workflows must continue to work identically in V2. Survey features are additive. Users who do not create survey campaigns should not see any UI changes except for new optional menu items or feature flags. This constraint ensures V2 can be deployed as an upgrade without breaking existing users' workflows or saved predictions. Database migrations must be backward-compatible (new tables and columns, no destructive changes to existing schema).

10. **Accessibility and Internationalization:** The surveyor mobile UI must meet WCAG 2.1 AA accessibility standards (keyboard navigation, screen reader support, color contrast ratios) to ensure usability for surveyors with disabilities. The UI is initially English-only (no internationalization), which limits adoption among non-English-speaking surveyors in states like Tamil Nadu, West Bengal, or Rajasthan where regional languages dominate. This is an accepted limitation for V2, with internationalization as a planned V3 feature. Survey coordinators in non-English states should recruit bilingual surveyors or provide translated training materials externally.

## Success Criteria

1. **Surveyor Workflow Functional:** A survey coordinator can create a survey campaign via API or admin UI, define booths and assign surveyors, and provide surveyor login credentials. A surveyor can log in on a mobile browser, view assigned booths, enter observations for at least eight standard factors (turnout estimate, incumbent support, challenger support, turnout delta from previous, voter enthusiasm, scheme satisfaction, candidate likeability, dominant local issue), attach one photo per booth, rate confidence (high/medium/low), and submit. The submission is stored in the database, appears in the coordinator's dashboard within 60 seconds, and triggers a prediction recalculation for that constituency if the booth is the 50th booth surveyed in that constituency (arbitrary threshold to avoid recomputing after every single submission). The surveyor can work offline, submit three booths while disconnected, and observe automatic sync when connectivity returns, with "pending sync" indicators replaced by "synced" confirmation.

2. **Booth-Level Prediction API Operational:** The GET `/v1/predict/booths/{constituency_no}` endpoint returns booth-level predictions for a constituency with 200-300 booths within three seconds. Each booth prediction includes booth_id, booth_name, predicted_winner, predicted_winner_share, predicted_runner_up, predicted_margin, and confidence interval. Booth predictions aggregate correctly to match constituency-level predictions within one percentage point of vote share (rounding tolerance). A backtesting validation on Tamil Nadu 2021 (if booth-level historical data is available) shows booth-level predictions achieve at least 70% winner accuracy at the booth level and 85% at the constituency level, demonstrating that booth granularity does not introduce excessive noise.

3. **Survey Dashboard Displays Real-Time Progress:** The `/surveys/dashboard/{campaign_id}` view loads within two seconds for a campaign with 1000 booths and 50 surveyors, displays completion percentage (booths surveyed / total booths), constituency-level breakdown with per-constituency completion, surveyor leaderboard with submissions count and quality scores, and live activity feed showing the five most recent submissions. The dashboard updates automatically via polling every 30 seconds without requiring manual page refresh. Data quality indicators flag booths where multiple surveyors submitted conflicting data (>10% difference in any factor value) with a "conflict" badge, and coordinators can click to review and resolve.

4. **Interactive Heat Map Renders State-Level View:** The constituency heat map component renders Tamil Nadu's 234 constituencies in under 500ms on a mid-range laptop (Intel i5 equivalent, 8GB RAM, Chrome browser), color-coded by completion percentage, with smooth zoom and pan interactions. Clicking a constituency opens a sidebar showing predictions, survey progress, booth count, and a "View Booth-Level Predictions" link that loads the booth breakdown. The map supports toggling between "Survey Progress," "Prediction Confidence," and "Swing Magnitude" color schemes without re-fetching data (pure client-side color re-mapping). The map is fully responsive on mobile (touch-friendly zoom, legible constituency labels) and accessible (all constituencies have aria-labels with constituency name, keyboard navigation works, color schemes pass WCAG contrast checks with a secondary pattern overlay for colorblind users).

5. **Scenario Comparison Shows Side-by-Side Differences:** An analyst can save two prediction configurations as "Scenario A: High Turnout" and "Scenario B: Low Turnout," navigate to the scenario comparison view, select both scenarios, and view a table showing per-party seat count differences (e.g., "Party X: 120 seats in A, 108 seats in B, Δ = -12") and vote share differences. Differences exceeding 5 seats or 3 percentage points are color-coded red (losses) or green (gains). A bar chart visualizes seat count by party for both scenarios side-by-side. The comparison view computes and displays in under one second for a state with 200+ constituencies (all computation is client-side from cached prediction results).

6. **Psephological Report Generates Successfully:** The POST `/v1/reports/generate` endpoint accepts a campaign_id, generates a 10-15 page PDF report for a state with 234 constituencies within 10 seconds (synchronous generation), and returns a signed URL valid for 7 days. The PDF includes all required sections (title, executive summary, methodology, state summary, constituency table, swing analysis, data provenance, disclaimer), is professionally formatted with consistent fonts and spacing, includes at least one chart (party seat distribution bar chart), and correctly aggregates survey data into summary statistics. The report is downloadable and viewable in browser PDF readers without errors. The report includes a data quality section stating "Survey data collected from 150 of 234 constituencies (64% coverage), average confidence rating: 7.8/10, 12 conflicts resolved."

7. **Booth-Level Predictions Integrate with Survey Data:** When a survey campaign has collected data for 50% or more of booths in a constituency, booth-level predictions for that constituency use survey observations as overrides for the corresponding factors. For example, if a surveyor reports 68% turnout estimate for Booth 42, the prediction engine uses 68% instead of the historical baseline turnout (say 62%) for that booth. The survey override is visibly indicated in the UI (booth predictions table shows "Survey: 68% [confidence: high]" vs. "Historical: 62%"). Predictions without survey data fall back to synthetic disaggregation. A validation test compares a constituency with 100% survey coverage (all booths surveyed) to a constituency with 0% survey coverage (pure historical baseline) and confirms that surveyed constituency predictions have narrower error margins (demonstrating that survey data reduces uncertainty).

8. **ML Model Includes Per-Prediction Explanations:** The `/v1/predict/ml` endpoint's response includes a new `feature_attributions` array for each constituency, listing the top five factors with their SHAP values (e.g., `[ {"factor": "turnoutChange", "shap_value": +2.3, "description": "Higher turnout favors challenger"}, ... ]`). The frontend displays these explanations in a collapsible "Why this prediction?" section below each constituency's result. SHAP computation adds less than 200ms to the total prediction latency (validated via load testing), ensuring the three-second response time target is still met.

9. **Conflict Resolution Workflow Functional:** When Surveyor A submits "turnout_estimate: 55%" for Booth 101 and Surveyor B submits "turnout_estimate: 62%" for the same booth within 24 hours, the system flags a conflict in the dashboard. The coordinator navigates to the conflicts view, sees both submissions with timestamps, confidence ratings, and attached photos, and selects "Use Surveyor A's value" or "Enter custom value: 58%." The resolution is saved to the database with a `resolved_by` and `resolution_timestamp` audit log entry. The prediction engine uses the resolved value. Future predictions no longer show the conflict indicator for that booth.

10. **Survey Submission Performance and Scalability:** A survey campaign with 50 active surveyors each submitting 20 booths per day (1000 submissions/day) processes all submissions without backend errors or timeout failures. The `/v1/surveys/booths/{booth_id}/submit` endpoint responds within 500ms per submission at the 95th percentile under load (tested with locust or k6 load testing tool simulating 50 concurrent users). The PostgreSQL database handles the write load without connection pool exhaustion (the default asyncpg pool size of 10-20 connections is sufficient). Survey dashboard queries remain under two seconds even with 10,000+ submissions in the campaign (indexed queries on `campaign_id`, `booth_id`, `surveyor_user_id`, `timestamp` columns).

11. **Offline-First Mobile UI Works Without Connectivity:** A surveyor enables airplane mode on their mobile device, opens the survey app (which has already loaded the campaign data while online), enters observations for three booths, clicks "Submit" for each, and sees "Pending sync (3)" indicator. The surveyor reconnects to Wi-Fi, and within 30 seconds observes the indicator change to "All synced ✓" without manual action. Refreshing the browser does not lose unsent submissions—they persist in IndexedDB. Attempting to submit more than 10 booths while offline shows a warning "You have 10 unsynced submissions. Please connect to sync before continuing" to prevent unlimited local storage growth.

12. **Data Quality Scores Reflect Submission Quality:** The system computes a quality score for each submission based on: (a) completeness (all mandatory fields filled = +20 points, missing fields = penalty), (b) consistency (values within expected ranges and not extreme outliers = +30 points), (c) confidence rating (surveyor self-rated confidence: high = +20, medium = +10, low = +5), (d) timeliness (submitted within 2 hours of GPS timestamp = +15, delayed = lower), (e) photo evidence (photo attached = +15). Maximum score is 100. Surveyors with average scores above 85 earn a "High Quality" badge displayed in the dashboard. Submissions scoring below 50 are automatically flagged for coordinator review. A validation test shows that submissions with quality scores above 80 have 15% lower variance in their factor values compared to submissions below 50 (demonstrating the score correlates with actual data reliability).

13. **Accessibility Compliance Verified:** The surveyor mobile UI passes axe-core automated accessibility tests with zero critical violations and fewer than five warnings. Manual screen reader testing (iOS VoiceOver or Android TalkBack) confirms that all form fields have appropriate labels, submission buttons are accessible, validation errors are announced, and navigation landmarks are present. Color contrast ratios for all text exceed WCAG AA thresholds (4.5:1 for normal text, 3:1 for large text). Keyboard navigation (tab order, focus indicators) works correctly for desktop users. The accessibility audit report is documented and any remaining warnings are triaged as "acceptable" or "fix in next iteration."

14. **Backward Compatibility Preserved:** After deploying V2, all existing V1 bookmarks (saved predictions with 12-factor sliders and alliance configurations) load correctly, produce identical predictions to V1 within 0.5% vote share tolerance (accounting for potential rounding differences from formula refactoring), and do not show survey-related UI elements unless the user creates a survey campaign. API endpoints `/v1/factors`, `/v1/predict/ml`, `/v1/predict/data` return the same response schemas as V1 (new fields are added but existing fields are not removed or renamed). Frontend users who do not have surveyor roles or do not create campaigns see the same V1 interface with minor additions (e.g., a new "Survey Campaigns" menu item that leads to a "No campaigns yet" empty state).

15. **Integration Testing End-to-End:** An automated Playwright or Cypress end-to-end test suite covers the complete surveyor workflow: (1) admin creates a campaign via API, (2) surveyor logs in on a mobile viewport, (3) selects campaign and booth, (4) enters survey data with all required fields, (5) submits successfully, (6) coordinator views submission in dashboard, (7) prediction for that constituency is requested and includes the survey data, (8) report is generated and downloaded. The test runs in under five minutes in CI, validates all assertions, and catches regressions in authentication, form validation, database writes, prediction computation, and report generation. This test serves as a high-confidence verification that the end-to-end system is functional before each release.

## Open Questions

1. **Booth Data Sourcing Priority:** Should the initial release prioritize synthetic booth-level disaggregation (simpler, works immediately for all states, but less accurate) or invest upfront effort in acquiring actual booth-level historical data for one pilot state like Tamil Nadu (more accurate, demonstrates feasibility, but delays release by 2-4 weeks for data acquisition and validation)? Recommendation: Start with synthetic disaggregation to unblock development, then integrate real booth data for Tamil Nadu as a Phase 1.5 enhancement post-initial release.

2. **Survey Questionnaire Customization Depth:** How much flexibility should survey campaigns allow in defining custom questionnaire fields beyond the standard twelve factors? Option A: Fixed questionnaire (only the twelve factors plus a free-text notes field)—simpler to implement, predictions are directly comparable across campaigns, but limits surveyor ability to collect contextual data. Option B: Fully customizable questionnaires (campaign creator defines arbitrary fields with types and ranges)—flexible, supports diverse research needs, but adds UI complexity for questionnaire builder and complicates prediction integration (custom fields cannot directly influence predictions unless mapped to factors). Recommendation: Option A for initial release, with Option B as a follow-on feature if user feedback indicates demand for custom fields.

3. **Real-Time Model Retraining Trigger Threshold:** At what volume of new survey data should the system trigger automatic model retraining? Options: (a) 1000 booth submissions, (b) 50% of constituencies surveyed, (c) manual trigger by admin, (d) nightly batch retraining job. Consideration: Retraining too frequently wastes compute and risks overfitting to early, potentially biased survey data; retraining too rarely means predictions don't benefit from accumulated survey insights. Recommendation: Start with option (c) manual trigger, allowing data scientists to inspect survey data quality before retraining, then automate with option (d) nightly batch job once confident in data quality.

4. **Booth-Level Prediction Exposure in Public UI:** Should booth-level predictions be publicly visible (with appropriate disclaimers) or restricted to authenticated users only? Risk: Booth-level predictions are less validated than constituency-level predictions, and publishing them could spread misinformation if inaccurate. Counter-argument: Transparency builds trust, and booth-level predictions are clearly marked with wide error margins. Recommendation: Restrict booth-level predictions to authenticated users in V2, then evaluate public exposure after validating accuracy on held-out elections in Phase 5.

5. **Social Media Sentiment vs. Survey-Collected Sentiment:** If social media sentiment integration is pursued, should it be a separate factor from survey-collected sentiment (surveyors asking voters "How do you feel about Party X?"), or should they be combined into a unified "Voter Sentiment" factor? Separate factors allow differentiating urban social media sentiment from rural ground sentiment, but add complexity. Combined factors simplify the UI but assume both signals measure the same underlying sentiment (questionable). Recommendation: Keep them separate as "Social Media Sentiment (Urban)" and "Ground Sentiment (Survey)" to make their distinct biases transparent.

6. **Conflict Resolution Default Behavior:** When surveyors submit conflicting data and no coordinator manually resolves it, what should the system do? Options: (a) Weighted average based on quality scores (automated, reduces coordinator burden, but may average two plausible values into an implausible midpoint). (b) Use the submission with higher quality score (simple, rewards good surveyors, but ignores the signal that a conflict exists). (c) Flag the booth as "unreliable" and exclude its data from predictions until resolved (conservative, avoids bad data, but reduces survey coverage). Recommendation: Option (a) weighted average with a visible "auto-resolved conflict" indicator so coordinators can review if desired.

7. **Photo Upload Moderation and Storage Costs:** Should photo uploads be mandatory, optional, or disabled by default? Photos provide evidence for data quality audits but increase storage costs (15GB/month for 100 surveyors as noted in constraints) and raise privacy concerns (booth photos may inadvertently capture voters or officials, violating privacy norms). Options: (a) Optional, surveyors can attach photos but are not required. (b) Mandatory, system refuses submission without a photo. (c) Disabled by default, campaign creator opts in. Recommendation: Option (a) optional, with privacy guidelines instructing surveyors to photograph booths without capturing individuals.

8. **Survey Campaign Visibility and Data Privacy:** Should survey campaigns default to private (only creator and assigned surveyors see data) or public (predictions visible to all users, but raw survey data redacted)? Legal and ethical considerations: If a political party runs a survey campaign, should opposition parties or journalists be able to see the resulting predictions? Privacy laws may restrict publishing voter-level data (though booth-level aggregates are likely permissible). Recommendation: Default to private with explicit opt-in for public predictions, and always redact surveyor names and raw responses from public views (only show aggregated predictions and summary statistics).

9. **Surveyor Incentives and Gamification:** Should the system include gamification (leaderboards, badges, progress bars) to motivate surveyors to complete booths? Argument for: Gamification increases engagement and completion rates. Argument against: Surveyors are typically paid professionals (field staff for campaigns, polling agencies, or news organizations) who do not need intrinsic motivation—gamification may feel patronizing or encourage gaming the metrics (e.g., submitting low-quality data quickly to rank higher). Recommendation: Implement basic leaderboards and quality badges for coordinator visibility (helps identify top performers for bonuses or recognition) but avoid overt game mechanics (points, achievements) unless user feedback from pilot deployments indicates demand.

10. **Booth-Level vs. Polling Station Terminology:** Indian electoral terminology uses "polling station" officially, but colloquial usage varies (some say "booth," some say "polling booth"). The system currently uses "booth" throughout. Should this be changed to "polling station" for official correctness, or kept as "booth" for colloquial familiarity? Recommendation: Use "polling station" in formal contexts (API documentation, PDF reports, legal disclaimers) and "booth" in user-facing UI (surveyor app, tooltips) where familiarity matters more than formality, with a one-time clarification that the terms are interchangeable.

11. **Error Margin Narrowing Logic:** How aggressively should error margins narrow as survey coverage increases? A naive approach: 0% coverage = historical error margin (say ±8pp), 100% coverage = minimal error margin (say ±2pp), linear interpolation. But this assumes survey data is high quality—if surveyors are submitting low-quality data, error margins should not narrow. Should error margin adjustment factor in data quality scores? Recommendation: Error margin = base_error × (1 - 0.7 × coverage_fraction × avg_quality_score / 100), where avg_quality_score is the mean quality score of all submissions for that constituency. This formula narrows margins as coverage and quality improve, but not to unrealistic levels.

12. **Development Index Granularity:** Development indices are typically published at district level, but constituencies are sub-district units in many states (one district may have 5-10 constituencies). How should district-level indices be mapped to constituencies? Options: (a) All constituencies in a district get the same index value (simple, but ignores intra-district variation). (b) Weight the district index by constituency type (urban constituencies get higher development scores than rural constituencies in the same district, using a uniform adjustment factor). (c) Manual mapping based on local knowledge (time-intensive, not scalable). Recommendation: Option (a) for initial release with a documented limitation, then Option (b) if data reveals large urban/rural development gaps.

13. **Prediction Update Frequency During Active Surveys:** When a campaign is active and surveyors are submitting data throughout the day, how often should constituency predictions update? Options: (a) After every submission (real-time, but computationally expensive and may produce volatile predictions as single booths swing the forecast). (b) After every 10 submissions per constituency (batched, reduces compute, but means early submissions have delayed impact). (c) On-demand when a user requests predictions (lazy, no background compute, but means dashboard may show stale predictions). Recommendation: Option (b) batched updates every 10 submissions, with a manual "Refresh Predictions" button for users who want to see the latest forecast immediately.

14. **Multi-Year Survey Campaign Tracking:** Should survey campaigns be single-use (one campaign per election year) or reusable (a coordinator creates a "Tamil Nadu Assembly Election" campaign template and clones it for each election cycle, preserving booth definitions and surveyor assignments)? Reusability saves setup time but requires versioning (booth boundaries may change due to delimitation). Recommendation: Single-use campaigns for V2 to avoid versioning complexity, with a "Duplicate Campaign" feature that copies configuration (booths, surveyors) to a new campaign for the next election, allowing manual edits before launch.

15. **Regulatory Compliance for Public Predictions:** If predictions are made publicly visible during an active election under the Model Code of Conduct, does the platform need to register as a survey organization or comply with exit poll regulations? This is a legal question requiring expert consultation. Recommendation: Until legal advice is obtained, default all campaigns to private during active MCC periods (dates between election announcement and result declaration), requiring explicit admin override to make predictions public, and prominently display legal disclaimers that the platform is an analytical tool, not an official poll.
